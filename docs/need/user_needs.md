# User Needs

## Target User Groups

### 1. Local Residents
**Who**: People living in Trondheim who are curious about their surroundings.

**Primary Needs**:
- "When was my building built?"
- "What did this area look like when my grandparents lived here?"
- "What buildings were demolished to make room for modern development?"

**Usage Pattern**: Occasional exploration, focused on specific locations (home, workplace, childhood neighborhoods).

**Success Indicators**:
- Can find their address easily
- Can understand the timeline without technical knowledge
- Feel emotionally connected to the history revealed

### 2. Historians and Researchers
**Who**: Academic researchers, local historians, genealogists.

**Primary Needs**:
- Access to temporal data for specific time periods
- Ability to verify building dates against multiple sources
- Export capabilities for use in research papers
- Transparency about data provenance and confidence

**Usage Pattern**: Deep exploration, systematic analysis, citation of specific sources.

**Success Indicators**:
- Can filter by data source (SEFRAK, Kartverket maps, etc.)
- Can assess data quality and confidence levels
- Can cite the tool in academic work
- Can export data or screenshots for documentation

### 3. Urban Planners and Architects
**Who**: Professionals working on development projects in Trondheim.

**Primary Needs**:
- Historical context for development sites
- Understanding of urban growth patterns
- Identification of potential cultural heritage sites
- Visual communication tool for stakeholder presentations

**Usage Pattern**: Project-specific research, presentation creation, pattern analysis.

**Success Indicators**:
- Can quickly assess historical significance of sites
- Can create compelling visualizations for presentations
- Can identify development patterns over time

### 4. Educators and Students
**Who**: Teachers and students studying local history, geography, or urban development.

**Primary Needs**:
- Interactive learning tool for teaching local history
- Visual comparison of different time periods
- Engaging way to explore geographic changes
- No technical barriers to entry

**Usage Pattern**: Classroom demonstrations, homework assignments, project research.

**Success Indicators**:
- Works reliably in school computer labs
- Intuitive enough for students to use independently
- Provides "wow moments" that engage learners
- Can be used without installation or account creation

### 5. Heritage Enthusiasts
**Who**: Members of local history societies, walking tour guides, cultural heritage advocates.

**Primary Needs**:
- Detailed information about historical buildings
- Ability to create custom walking tour routes
- Sharing capabilities for social media or blog posts
- Discovery of lesser-known historical features

**Usage Pattern**: Regular exploration, content creation, community engagement.

**Success Indicators**:
- Discover new historical insights regularly
- Can share specific views with others
- Tool becomes a resource for tour planning

## Common Needs Across User Groups

### Accessibility
- Works on desktop and mobile devices
- No special software or account required
- Intuitive interface with minimal learning curve
- Accessible to users with disabilities

### Trust and Transparency
- Clear indication of data sources
- Visible confidence levels for uncertain data
- Attribution to original data providers
- Acknowledgment of gaps and limitations

### Performance
- Loads quickly on standard internet connections
- Smooth interaction and responsive controls
- Works reliably across browsers
- Handles zooming and panning without lag

### Discovery
- Easy to find interesting historical changes
- Ability to explore randomly or search specifically
- Suggested "points of interest" or notable changes
- Context about what they're seeing

## User Journey Examples

### Example 1: The Curious Resident
1. Opens the map, centered on Trondheim
2. Searches for their address
3. Adjusts time slider to see when their building appeared
4. Explores neighborhood to see what else has changed
5. Shares discovery with family members

### Example 2: The Researcher
1. Navigates to area of interest
2. Selects specific time period (e.g., 1880-1920)
3. Filters to show only SEFRAK buildings
4. Cross-references with Kartverket map source
5. Takes notes on buildings with high confidence data
6. Exports findings for research paper

### Example 3: The Educator
1. Prepares lesson on industrial development
2. Identifies key time periods (pre/post railway arrival)
3. Creates screenshots showing before/after
4. Demonstrates live in classroom
5. Students explore independently for homework

## Unmet Needs and Future Considerations

### Current Limitations
- Cannot contribute corrections or additional information
- No social/community features
- Limited contextual information (why buildings were demolished, historical events)
- No integration with historical photographs
- No 3D visualization

### Potential Future Features
- User-contributed historical photographs
- Annotations and storytelling layers
- Integration with archival records
- Virtual tours through historical streetscapes
- Community discussion around specific locations
